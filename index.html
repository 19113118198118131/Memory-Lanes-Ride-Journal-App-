Corner Speed vs Radius

Each dot is a detected corner: how tight it was (radius) against your apex speed.
The dashed curves are constant-grip lines at 0.2, 0.4 and 0.6 g, so you can see how much of the available grip you use in tight corners versus open sweepers.
Click a corner to jump the map to that moment.

How to read this

Dots low and left of a grip line mean you are using less of that grip level. If your dots hug the 0.4 g line in open sweepers but sit far below it in tight corners, you are more confident in fast bends than slow ones, which is common. Consistent vertical spread at one radius means similar corners taken at very different speeds. None of this says "go faster": it shows where your comfort zone sits so you can decide what to practise.

Acceleration Profile

Smoothed acceleration (m/s²) over distance. Red bands are detected braking zones, green bands are drive zones: a glance shows the rhythm of where you worked the controls.

How to read this

The blue line is how hard you were accelerating or decelerating. Red bands are braking zones, green bands are drive zones. A ride with clean rhythm shows tidy alternation: brake, corner, drive, cruise. Long red bands ending right where a corner starts suggest braking finished late. Spiky, ragged shapes inside a band suggest abrupt inputs.

Grip Usage (g-g diagram)

Every dot is a moment of the ride: cornering force left/right, braking and drive up/down.
The shape of the cloud is your riding signature. Riders who brake only upright draw a plus sign; trail braking fills the lower corners. This is a picture, not a score: there is nothing to maximise here.

How to read this

Left/right is cornering force, down is braking, up is drive. A plus-sign shaped cloud means you brake and accelerate only when upright, then corner without much overlap: the classic safe road style. Points in the lower corners of the circle mean braking while leaned over (trail braking). A tight, symmetric cloud is a smooth rider; a scattered one suggests abrupt inputs. The dashed circles are reference levels, not targets.
